About Biomimicry and Anatomy

As part of my undergraduate research project, I developed several simulations to help students learn anatomy as early as possible. Anatomy has a large variety of uses, one of which is biomimicry. The goal of this project was to raise awareness of anatomy, and to help streamline the learning process in a stimulating fashion. The main research questions was, "how does one teach something that is arguably mundane, like anatomy, in a engaging manner?" The solution I came up with was games.

The first simulation was a simulation on muscles. The idea behind this simulation was very simple. The program highlights a certain muscle, and the user must select the correct answer. Finish before the person next to you, and you have bragging rights.

The second simulation was inspired by the Hasbro game "Perfection." The whole idea was to fill in the silhouette of the skeleton with the correct piece, as quickly as possible. Unlike "Perfection" there is no time limit, and the pieces don't pop out. Anatomy can be hard and I didn't want to discourage anyone from learning it.

The third simulation is what I called the "Skeletal Playground." These simulations were supplements to "lessons" and in this lesson, I speak about how certain technologies in our society are based on human anatomy. An example I give is robots. I also mention the Eiffel Tower as a architectural form of biomimicry. The goal of this simulation is to allow users to go in, and essentially manipulate bones and muscles to their own liking, and to create their own work.
